 1. Data Parallelism (DP): "The Factory Line"
  In DP, you take your entire model and copy-paste it onto every GPU.
   * The Strategy: You split the Data (the batch). If you have 8 GPUs and a
     batch size of 64, each GPU gets 8 sentences.
   * The Math: Every GPU calculates its own gradients independently. At the
     end of the step, they all "talk" to each other (All-Reduce) to average
     their gradients and update the weights.
   * The "Why" (System Side): DP is the easiest to implement. It is great
     for increasing Throughput (how many tokens per second you can process).
   * The "Limit": The model must fit inside a single GPU’s memory. If your
     model is 20GB and your GPU only has 16GB, DP is impossible.

  ---

  2. Tensor Parallelism (TP): "The Large Object"
  In TP, you take a single matrix multiplication and split it across your
  GPUs.
   * The Strategy: You split the Weights (the tensors).
       * Example: For a Linear layer $Y = XW$, GPU 0 holds the left half of
         $W$, and GPU 1 holds the right half.
   * The Math: Both GPUs do the calculation simultaneously on the same data.
     They then "merge" their partial results (All-Gather or All-Reduce)
     during the forward pass.
   * The "Why" (System Side): TP allows you to train models that are larger
     than a single GPU. It also reduces Latency (the time it takes to
     process a single token) because multiple chips are working on the same
     calculation.
   * The "Limit": TP requires massive communication bandwidth (like NVLink).
     Because GPUs must talk during every layer, if your
     internet/interconnect is slow, TP will be much slower than DP.
